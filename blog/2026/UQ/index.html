Does Physics AI halluciate?: Two necessary safeguards for engineering decision making with Physics AI.

Created on May 30, 2026

2026

Does Physics AI Hallucinate?

Yes, it does.

At the end of the day, a trained neural network is a series of linear calculations and activation functions. There is no mechanism that will make it fail—unless the input is out of distribution or out of floating-point range. It will always give you something. And most of the time, that something will look plausible.

The Quiet Danger of Plausible Nonsense

Say you have a model trained on a midsize SUV for external aerodynamics. What happens if you run a full-size pickup truck through the model? You’ll get a pressure field. You’ll get a drag coefficient. The output will look like a perfectly reasonable CFD result.

But would you use that inference for an engineering decision?

In conventional CFD, unreliable results announce themselves—divergence, poor residuals, unphysical flow features that experienced engineers can spot. Machine learning surrogates offer no such courtesy. A prediction on an out-of-distribution geometry is visually indistinguishable from one on in-distribution data. The model is confidently wrong, and it has no idea.

Physics AI Needs Guardrails

Without proper guardrails, any pipeline built on Physics AI carries a high risk of failure at validation—the point where you test the ML-informed engineering design against real CAE simulation. That’s wasted time, wasted compute, and wasted engineering effort.

Proper guardrails can save valuable resources by:

  1. Filtering out meaningless inferences before you commit to acting on them, and
  2. Adding confidence intervals to the inference results so engineers know where to trust—and where not to.

Bracketing Inference with Uncertainty

At General Motors Research and Development, we have a (in terms of SciML timeline) long history with Scientific Machine Learning and Physics AI. Based on our experience deploying neural surrogates for production vehicle aerodynamics, we came to a clear conclusion: it is time to look at the uncertainties.

We examined two UQ methods and propose sandwiching any inference between them:

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  1. PRE-INFERENCE: Is this geometry in-domain?  │
│     → Mahalanobis Distance (OOD Detection)      │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  2. INFERENCE                                   │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  3. POST-INFERENCE: How confident is this?      │
│     → MC Dropout (Confidence Intervals)         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Stage 1: Before You Predict—Is This Geometry Even Appropriate?

Before committing GPU-hours to inference, a single forward pass through the model’s geometry encoder extracts a learned feature vector. We then compute the Mahalanobis distance between that vector and the training distribution’s statistics. If the distance exceeds the training distribution’s threshold, the geometry is flagged as out-of-distribution. Full stop. Run a CFD simulation instead.

In our experiments across 511 vehicle simulations spanning five vehicle types, this approach provided zero-overlap separation between seen and unseen vehicle types. The unseen vehicle scored 3× higher than any training sample—despite its drag coefficient and frontal area overlapping with the training vehicles in conventional scalar metrics. The feature space sees what Cd × Area plots cannot.

Even within the in-distribution regime, higher Mahalanobis distances consistently correlated with larger prediction errors—making it not just a binary gate, but a continuous reliability indicator.

Cost: One forward pass + a matrix–vector product. Negligible.

Stage 2: After You Predict—Where Should You Trust It?

For geometries that pass the OOD screen, MC Dropout provides spatially resolved uncertainty maps. By running 30 stochastic forward passes with dropout active at inference time, we obtain a distribution of predictions at every surface point. The variance tells you where on the vehicle the model is least confident.

The spatial signal is real: high-variance regions coincide with high-error regions (front fascia, wheel arches, underbody separation zones). This gives engineers actionable guidance—”look more carefully at this area”—rather than a single number for the whole vehicle.

An important caveat: The absolute confidence intervals from MC Dropout are severely miscalibrated. Our 95% CI achieved actual coverage of only 3.5–47.7% depending on the force component. The model is systematically overconfident. MC Dropout tells you where to worry, but not how much to worry. We report this honestly because practitioners need to know the limitations before deploying any UQ method.

What We Learned

  Mahalanobis Distance MC Dropout
Purpose Pre-inference OOD screening Post-inference confidence
Cost 1 forward pass 30× forward passes
Output Single score per geometry Spatially resolved field
OOD detection Direct, unambiguous Indirect (elevated σ)
Calibration N/A (it’s a score) Poor (overconfident)
Practical value High—deploy on every sample Moderate—reserve for critical decisions

The Takeaway

The contribution here is not algorithmic—neither Mahalanobis distance nor MC Dropout is new. The point is that SciML inference should be bracketed by uncertainty quantification, and that such a pipeline can be assembled from off-the-shelf components under realistic industrial constraints: limited simulation budgets, proprietary geometries, and models with high-dimensional feature spaces.

As neural surrogates move from benchmark papers toward production engineering tools, uncertainty quantification is not an optional add-on. It is the missing piece that turns an impressive demo into a deployable system.

The pipeline structure—pre-inference OOD screening followed by post-inference confidence estimation—is not specific to aerodynamics. Any SciML surrogate that maps geometry to physical fields faces the same risk of silent extrapolation. Crashworthiness, thermal management, structural analysis—wherever data-driven surrogates support engineering decisions, they need guardrails.

In my last post, I wrote about the transition from CAE to AAE — AI-Aided Engineering. I ended with a question I left hanging on purpose.

What is the role of an engineer in the age of AAE?

Let me tell you how I got to my answer.

It started with vibe coding. Simple prompts, instant scripts. Then I started automating the automation — agents that gather data, prepare inputs, run ML training, analyze results, write reports, and then design and kick off the next round of experiments on their own.

The speed of thought is now lightspeed. Literally. I can't keep up with the reasoning. I have to open the thinking steps and read through them just to understand what Claude did — and that reading alone takes longer than the AI's entire reasoning process, which already includes writing scripts, gathering and processing data. What would have taken me hours, if not days, is done before I finish my coffee.

But there is one thing Claude doesn't have. That I have.

Curiosity.

LLMs are great — often perfect — at executing a given task. They occasionally hallucinate, which is on me to catch. Sometimes they give up, which is actually a new behavior — two years ago they would do anything to satisfy you, even lie. But here's what I notice: when a task is done, the LLM is done. Its analysis is thorough within the given boundary. But it never wonders what if. It doesn't get curious about what lies just outside the frame.

That gives the engineer enormous freedom.

Conventional CAE required years of painstaking mastery — the physics, the tools, the art of meshing, the instinct for postprocessing. I still remember staying up through cold nights writing my first k-ε turbulence code for a driven cavity in FORTRAN. Not anymore. The LLM handles that. But it lacks the ideation.

That's it. Ideation. From curiosity.

That's what LLMs don't have yet. And with LLMs handling the execution, any engineer can now become an architect — not a quick PoC on a simplified setup, but full-fidelity evaluation of a new idea, fast. The LLM does it for you. Democratization of tools. Ideas get tested at their fullest fidelity, at the speed of thought.

That is the role of the engineer in AAE. Only the creative ones — the curious ones — will thrive.

Which is why I find it absurd when companies fire engineers and programmers hoping to replace them with AI. AI gives freedom and speed. It lets engineers do more, faster. But without a curious engineer driving the questions, it's just a very fast answer to a question nobody asked.

The right expectation is: my engineers will do five months of work in one month. Not: I need one engineer with AI instead of five. That's not a productivity insight. That's a layoff dressed up as innovation.

AI means something only with a curious engineer — the Human in the Loop.

That is, until AI becomes curious. I hope that day doesn't come too soon.
